# Upstate Basketball League Brand Identity

## Brand core

**Purpose:** Give growing varsity basketball programs structured, meaningful competition that honors God, builds character, and connects communities.

**Positioning:** The Upstate Basketball League is a faith-rooted league for smaller schools, homeschool programs, church-affiliated programs, and independent teams that want dependable organization, strong competition, shared standards, and a championship experience worth pursuing.

**Brand promise:** How we win matters.

**Core expression:** Faith. Competition. Kingdom impact.

**Primary audiences:**

- Student-athletes and their families.
- Coaches, athletic directors, and designated program representatives.
- Schools, homeschool organizations, churches, and independent programs evaluating the league.
- Officials, volunteers, supporters, and local basketball communities.

**Primary user goals:** Find schedules, standings, teams, rules, and postseason information quickly. Prospective programs should understand the league's standards and know how to contact league leadership.

## Personality

UBL should feel:

- **Faith-centered:** belief is visible through preparation, integrity, humility, service, and conduct.
- **Competitive:** games matter, excellence is expected, and the championship should feel earned.
- **Disciplined:** schedules, standards, officiating, and communication feel dependable.
- **Respectful:** intensity never becomes arrogance, hostility, or humiliation.
- **Community-built:** every program contributes to something larger than one team or result.
- **Upward-looking:** the league helps young athletes and growing programs become stronger over time.

UBL should not feel like professional-league cosplay, an aggressive combat brand, an esports product, a generic church flyer, or a youth program that treats competition as an afterthought.

## Voice and copy

- Write with short, active, confident sentences.
- Pair competitive language with character: “Compete hard. Walk humbly. Honor God.”
- Integrate faith through actions and standards rather than vague inspirational language.
- Use basketball language naturally: opening night, starting five, tipoff, seed, bracket, possession, championship path.
- Give operational information plainly. Dates, locations, eligibility, schedule changes, and league decisions must be unambiguous.
- Use “we,” “our,” “programs,” and “student-athletes” to reinforce shared ownership.
- Avoid trash talk, war metaphors, exaggerated promises, insider jargon, and language that divides programs into insiders and outsiders.
- When quoting Scripture, preserve the wording accurately and identify the translation.

## Message hierarchy

1. **Faith:** Honor God in how we prepare, compete, lead, win, and lose.
2. **Competition:** Prepare well, play hard, and pursue victory with integrity.
3. **Character:** Let both victory and defeat reveal maturity and humility.
4. **Trust:** Communicate clearly and administer the league consistently.
5. **Legacy:** Build a foundation that serves athletes and programs for years.

## Visual system

### Color

| Role | Token | Value | Use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Midnight navy | `--navy-950` | `#020f22` | Primary dark field, headers, arena atmosphere |
| Deep navy | `--navy-900` | `#041a38` | Panels and secondary dark surfaces |
| League blue | `--navy-800` | `#073467` | Branded fields, active information, structure |
| Bright blue | `--navy-700` | `#0b4b89` | Controlled highlights and division accents |
| UBL red | `--red` | `#e31331` | Primary action, urgency, active states, key rules |
| Deep red | `--red-dark` | `#bc0d27` | Hover states and tonal depth |
| White | `--white` | `#ffffff` | High-contrast type and clean space |
| Canvas | `--canvas` | `#eef2f7` | Page background and neutral data surfaces |
| Ink | `--ink` | `#07172d` | Primary type on light surfaces |
| Muted | `--muted` | `#637084` | Supporting data and secondary copy |

Blue and red are the competitive identity; midnight navy and white keep it credible. Championship gold may appear in trophies, medals, or ceremonial imagery, but it is not a general interface accent and should not compete with UBL red.

### Typography

- **Display:** Barlow Condensed, weights 600-900, for headlines, team names, section titles, and high-energy calls to action.
- **Body and UI:** IBM Plex Sans, weights 400-700, for schedules, standings, rules, navigation, and explanatory copy.
- **Scoreboard numerals:** Teko, weights 600-700, only where a true scoreboard, bracket, seed, clock, or large numeric treatment benefits from it.
- Use uppercase selectively for sports-display moments. Long paragraphs and operational information should remain in normal case.
- Keep scores, dates, abbreviations, labels, and table values aligned and immediately scannable.

### Logo

- Use the supplied UBL logo files as the only source of the primary mark. Do not redraw, reinterpret, or regenerate the player silhouette with an image model.
- Preserve the original blue/red split, white silhouette, proportions, and clear space.
- Use the optimized mark for navigation and small UI; use the full-resolution transparent asset only when the larger format requires it.
- Keep the mark on white, midnight navy, or a quiet neutral field. Do not place it over busy action photography without a controlled solid container.
- Do not add flames, wings, crowns, crosses, basketball seams, bevels, or extra glow to the logo.

The current mark can be read as an upward jump shot representing effort and aspiration, a blue/red field representing competition, and a white athlete representing one shared league standard above individual teams. Because the split field and white player silhouette also resemble familiar professional-league conventions, treat the supplied mark as the current asset and consider a distinctiveness/trademark review before expanding it to merchandise or broad commercial use.

### Layout and components

- Build from court geometry: center circles, baselines, bracket paths, score columns, roster rows, and strong red/blue divisions.
- Favor clear rectangular panels, crisp rules, and compact scoreboard-like groupings. Rounded containers should have a specific role rather than becoming the default.
- Utility content comes first. Schedule, standings, rules, and team information must remain faster to read than any decorative treatment.
- Use strong display headlines with disciplined spacing. Avoid excessive badges, decorative pills, fake stat labels, and empty card grids.
- Team logos must use `object-fit: contain`, retain their native proportions, and receive equal visual respect regardless of original shape.

## Image art direction: Court & Calling

UBL uses two clearly separated image modes.

### Documentary photography

- Real game, team, coach, official, and community photographs document actual UBL people and events.
- Favor effort, huddles, sportsmanship, coaching, officiating, preparation, and postgame respect alongside peak action.
- Preserve natural gym color, real texture, and recognizable context. Crop for emotion without misrepresenting what happened.
- Never generate replacement documentary photos of fictional UBL games, players, coaches, trophies, or crowds.
- Confirm publication permission for documentary images, especially photographs showing student-athletes who may be minors, before public launch or promotional reuse.

### Brand illustration

- Generated imagery must be visibly illustrative: premium sports-editorial collage, screen-print texture, halftone grain, cut-paper silhouettes, court-line geometry, and controlled arena light.
- Use anonymous, non-identifiable figures with blank uniforms and no team marks or readable numbers.
- Build primarily with midnight navy, UBL blue, UBL red, white, and restrained warm-gold light.
- The work should feel reverent, competitive, and communal without becoming solemn, violent, or theatrical.
- Generated art may communicate principles and atmosphere; it must never be captioned as a real game or real participant.

### Avoid

- Fake documentary photography, invented team uniforms, readable jersey numbers, fabricated sponsors, or generated versions of real athletes.
- NBA-like broadcast graphics, glossy esports neon, flames, lightning, smoke explosions, mascots, generic church stock art, or oversized religious symbols.
- Illegible image-generated text, altered logos, watermarks, malformed hands, impossible basketballs, or crowds with distorted faces.

## GPT Image Combinations: Faith / Competition / Kingdom Impact

The generated triptych should feel like one campaign told in three beats:

1. **Faith / Before the whistle:** A quiet, anonymous athlete silhouette at center court beneath disciplined arena light. A subtle cross is suggested only by intersecting light and court geometry. The feeling is preparation, humility, and purpose.
2. **Competition / Give your best:** Two anonymous athlete silhouettes contest the ball in a dynamic red/blue editorial composition. The feeling is effort, integrity, balance, and earned intensity—not hostility.
3. **Kingdom Impact / Beyond one team:** An overhead anonymous huddle of athletes, coaches, and community figures connected around the center circle. Court lines extend outward as paths, expressing service, trust, and lasting influence.

All three images should use the same collage construction, silhouette treatment, halftone grain, arena lighting, court-line weight, and palette. Keep important action inside a center-safe area so the files can support desktop landscapes and tighter mobile crops. Do not include text or the UBL logo inside the generated art.

## Motion

- Motion should feel like a scoreboard update, bracket advance, whistle-to-action transition, or arena reveal.
- Use short number changes, line draws, panel wipes, table-row emphasis, and restrained image reveals.
- Keep data stable while it is being read. Never animate live scores, standings, or rules so aggressively that users lose context.
- Avoid long intro sequences, continuous background motion, heavy parallax, bouncing UI, and effects that imply a live update when data has not changed.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`; all information and controls must remain complete without animation.

## Accessibility and responsive rules

- Maintain WCAG AA contrast and visible keyboard focus.
- Do not rely on red versus blue alone to distinguish divisions, outcomes, or state. Pair color with text, position, iconography, or pattern.
- Use semantic tables for standings and schedules where appropriate, with useful headings and mobile alternatives that preserve context.
- Keep touch targets at least 44px, prevent horizontal page overflow, and make tables intentionally scrollable only when a stacked presentation would lose meaning.
- Use useful alt text for documentary photography. Decorative brand illustrations should use empty alt text when the adjacent copy already communicates their meaning.
- Test at 1440px desktop, 768px tablet, and 390px mobile, plus narrow widths for long school names, dates, and score rows.

## Decision filter

Before approving a UBL design choice, ask:

1. Does it honor faith through conduct rather than decoration?
2. Does it make competition feel meaningful and credible?
3. Can a family, coach, or program representative find the practical information quickly?
4. Does it respect real teams and avoid fabricating league history?
5. Does it strengthen character, trust, or community?
6. Would it still work clearly without color or motion?
